Lesson 1: Everything About Phishing

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyberattack where attackers send fake messages that appear to come from a trusted or well-known source — usually through email or text messages (SMS). These messages are designed to trick the recipient into giving away sensitive information, such as login credentials, personal data, or financial details.

Phishing attacks can also be used to:

  • Install malware on a user's device,
  • Steal money or identities,
  • Or gain access to company networks and confidential data.

"Last year Cisco Security saw that 80% of ransomware attacks we observed began with a phishing email," said
Tom Gillis SVP and GM Security at Cisco, RSA Conference 2023

How do phishing scams trick users?

Phishing scams are built on social engineering — attackers craft fake messages that look like they're from trusted sources. These emails or texts may seem harmless but are meant to lure users into dangerous actions, such as:

  • Clicking on malicious links,
  • Downloading harmful attachments,
  • Or entering login details on fake websites.

Why do hackers send phishing scams?

Attackers often target personally identifiable information (PII) or login credentials to access accounts and commit fraud. With enough information, cybercriminals may:

  • Drain bank accounts,
  • Compromise company systems,
  • Or even launch ransomware attacks that lock data unless a ransom is paid.

How does phishing work?

Phishing succeeds by imitating trust. Victims receive messages that seem urgent or emotionally persuasive, asking them to click links or enter personal information on fake sites that look real

Tactics include:

  • Fear ("Your account will be locked!"),
  • Curiosity ("You've won a prize!"),
  • Urgency ("Update your details now!"),
  • Or impersonation of trusted individuals or brands.

Some phishing campaigns even target specific people (known as spear phishing) using custom messages. It only takes one mistake to let attackers in — that's why it's critical to Think Before You Click. (You can click that link, as it has important phishing stats and information.)

Who are the targets of phishing?

Everyone is a potential target. Phishing attackers usually don't pick specific people—they send out mass emails or messages hoping that even a few recipients will fall for the scam. Because of this broad targeting, it's essential that all users are educated through security awareness training. Knowing what phishing looks like—and how to respond—can make a huge difference in preventing security breaches.

Why are phishing so effective?

Phishing works so well because it preys on human behavior. It takes advantage of:

  • Our tendency to trust people or messages that look official,
  • Our curiosity about unexpected emails,
  • And our impulsive responses to messages that seem urgent or emotional.

On top of that, phishing is cheap and easy for attackers to launch:

  • Bulk email lists are easy to buy.
  • Phishing kits (ready-made scam tools) are available on the dark web.
  • Sending emails costs almost nothing.
  • The risks for the attacker are low—but the damage to the victim can be huge.

That's why staying alert and thinking critically before clicking links or opening attachments is one of your best defenses.

Lesson 2: Dangers of phishing - What can happen when you are phished?

Phishing can have serious consequences for individuals and organizations.

For Individual:

  • Bank funds may be stolen
  • Unauthorized charges may appear on your credit cards
  • You could lose access to important files and photos
  • Hackers might take over your social media
  • Criminals could impersonate you and target your family or friends

For organizations:

  • Loss of company funds
  • Leakage of personal data belonging to clients, partners, or staff
  • Disruption of operations due to locked files or systems
  • Damage to company reputation and trust

Phishing Awareness & Prevention

Phishing attacks evolve quickly, so it's important to stay informed.

What your organization should do:

  • Use email filters and security software
  • Train employees regularly to spot and report phishing
  • Run phishing simulations to test alertness

Personal safety tips:

  • Don't click on suspicious email links or attachments
  • Keep software and browsers updated
  • Don't share sensitive information through email or unfamiliar websites
  • Be cautious with emotional or urgent messages
  • Regularly review your accounts for suspicious activity
